Analyzing a policy is akin to a forensic audit of intent, language, and outcome. For a researcher in your field, it moves beyond "what the document says" and into the mechanics of how that document functions as a lever of power.

Here are the primary mechanics of policy analysis, broken down from a scholarly perspective:

---

### 1. Problem Definition (The Framing Mechanic)

Policy analysis doesn't start with the solution; it starts with how the "problem" is constructed.

- **The "WPR" Approach:** Carol Bacchi’s "What’s the Problem Represented to be?" framework is a standard mechanic. If a policy frames the "problem" as _disruptive student behavior_, the analysis reveals that the solution will naturally be _exclusion_. If the problem is framed as _lack of institutional support_, the solution changes to _resource allocation_.
    

### 2. The Narrative & Linguistic Audit

This involves a "close reading" of the policy’s language to identify the "hidden curriculum."

- **Discretionary Language:** Mechanics focus on words like _"may," "should,"_ or _"at the principal's discretion."_ These are the friction points where bias often enters the system.
    
- **Passive vs. Active Voice:** Analyzing who is the "actor" and who is "acted upon." (e.g., _"The student was suspended"_ vs. _"The administration chose to suspend"_).
    

### 3. Implementation Logic (The "Gears")

This looks at the mechanical link between the written rule and the boots-on-the-ground action.

- **The Theory of Change:** Does the policy actually have a logical pathway to its stated goal? If the goal is "Safety" but the mechanic is "Out-of-school suspension," the analysis checks the evidence-base for whether removing a student actually increases total school safety.
    
- **Administrative Burden:** Calculating the "cost" of compliance. Who has to fill out the forms? Who has to prove their innocence? Often, the mechanics of a policy are designed to be so tedious that they discourage people from seeking the protections the policy supposedly offers.
    

### 4. Quantitative Impact (The Empirical Mechanic)

This is where you apply the "Disproportionality" lens you've been focusing on.

- **Descriptive Statistics:** Using R or Python to run "Risk Ratios." (e.g., If Student Group A represents 10% of the population but 40% of the suspensions, the policy mechanic is failing at equity).
    
- **Trend Analysis:** Looking at data before and after a policy change to see if the "pivot" actually shifted the needle or just rebranded the harm.
    

### 5. Stakeholder Mapping

Analyzing the "Who."

- **The Power Matrix:** Identifying who benefits from the policy (e.g., contractors, administrators) and who bears the cost (e.g., underserved students, families). This mechanic reveals why "bad" policies often persist—they are usually working perfectly for the people in power.
